Imagine you need to find the derivative of the function \(x sin(x)\). An obvious way to think about this derivative is using the fundamental definition of derivatives. That is
\[\frac{d(x sin(x))}{dx}=lim_{h\rightarrow0}\frac{(x+h)sin(x+h)-xsin(x)}{h}\]
As much as fun it is to compute these limits, the complexities quickly spiral out of control. To figure out a way to solve this problem of finding derivatives of products, we use the rule intuitively names the product rule.
The Product Rule - Formula

\[\frac{d(\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x)})}{dx}=\color{red}{\frac{df}{dx}}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x)}+\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{\frac{dg}{dx}}\]
In a nutshell, the product rule says that the derivative of two functions is the sum of the products of derivative of one function with the other function. Let us take an example, finding the derivative of the function \(xsin(x)\).
\[\frac{\color{red}{x}\color{blue}{sin(x)}}{dx}=\color{red}{\frac{d(x)}{dx}}\cdot\color{blue}{sin(x)}+\color{red}{(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{\frac{d(sin(x))}{dx}}\]
We know that the derivative of x with respect to itself is just 1 and the derivative of sin (x) with respect to x is cos(x), that is \(\color{red}{\frac{d(x)}{dx}=1}\) and \(\color{blue}{\frac{d(sin(x))}{dx}=cos(x)}\). Now just put everything in the expression and we would get
\[\frac{\color{red}{x}\color{blue}{sin(x)}}{dx}=\color{red}{1}\cdot\color{blue}{sin(x)}+\color{red}{(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{cos(x)}=sin(x)+xcos(x)\]

Proving the Product Rule

The only formal tool we have in our arsenal to prove something about derivatives is the very definition of a derivative. Let's try to prove this useful rule using the definition. In the course of this definition, we would assume that the functions \(\color{red}{f}\) and \(\color{blue}{g}\) are differentiable an thus continuous.
\[\frac{d(\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x)})}{dx}=lim_{h\rightarrow0}\frac{\color{red}{f(x+h)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x+h)}-\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x)}}{h}\]
There's not a whole lot that we can do with an expression like this (you might be tempted to put in the value of h to be zero in the expression but you would just get \(\frac{0}{0}\) just like in the case of all derivatives). Instead let's try manipulating the fraction to get something a little more insightful. The popular trick of adding and subtracting comes to our rescue here. If we add and subtract \(\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x+h)}\) or \(\color{red}{f(x+h)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x)}\) in the numerator, it breaks down the derivative into easier manageable components. In this case let's try subtracting and adding \(\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x+h)}\) while you can try the other one as an exercise.
\[\frac{d(\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x)})}{dx}=lim_{h\rightarrow0}\frac{\color{red}{f(x+h)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x+h)}-\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x+h)}+\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x+h)}-\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x)}}{h}\]
Let us now group similar terms
\[\frac{d(\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x)})}{dx}=lim_{h\rightarrow0}[\color{blue}{g(x+h)}(\frac{\color{red}{f(x+h)-f(x)}}{h})+\color{red}{f(x)}(\frac{\color{blue}{g(x+h)-g(x)}}{h})]\]
If we now evaluate this limit, we can observe that \(lim_{h\rightarrow0}\color{blue}{g(x+h)}=g(x)\), \(lim_{h\rightarrow0}\color{red}{\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}}=\frac{df}{dx}\) and \(lim_{h\rightarrow0}\color{blue}{\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}}=\frac{dg}{dx}\). And that's it, put in everything and we have the product rule.
\[\frac{d(\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x)})}{dx}=\color{red}{\frac{df}{dx}}\cdot\color{blue}{g(x)}+\color{red}{f(x)}\cdot\color{blue}{\frac{dg}{dx}}\]
